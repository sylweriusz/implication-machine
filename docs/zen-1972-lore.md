# THE PLUMB LINE AND THE PAINTED CAKE
### Notes toward a Zen of Making
*transcribed for the seminar in Mind & Craft — Spring, 1972*

*A handout. Read it slowly. Where it seems to speak of building it speaks of seeing; where it seems to speak of seeing, you are building.*

---

### The One Rule

The master Tetsu said to the seminary: *speak not of the water until you have drunk.*

"Done" is a word. "Right" is a word. "Allowed" is a word. A word is a finger; the moon is elsewhere. Nothing you say of a thing is true until the thing, in the world, has shown it so — and shown it in a way another could walk out and witness for himself.

A student asked, "Then what may I trust?"
"What you can taste twice," said the master.

He held up two fingers. *One hand asks: does it do what it should? The other asks: what was it permitted, and what did it do?* These are not two practices. They are one barrier approached from two sides, and both are written in the same dust — the track of what was actually done.

---

### First Gate · The Living Vow

The vow is not poured full at dawn. You write it as you walk, and each step that surprises you, you add to it. A scroll that was finished before the road is a lie dressed as foresight; a scroll that fills from the road is alive.

Do not freeze it. To revise the vow is to ripen it, never to fail it.

*The ox is not bought.*
*He is glimpsed, then tracked, then known.*
*The rope comes last.*

---

### Second Gate · The Painted Cake

A painted rice cake does not satisfy hunger.

When you would prove a thing, prove the thing itself: real water, real fire, the real weight in the real hand. A trial against your own picture of the world tells you only that your picture agrees with itself — and a picture has never once been hungry.

A student, hurried, set a painted cake before the master and said the work was done. The master studied it a long while. "It is a very good painting," he said. The student rose and went to the well.

Mark this: a real trial that *fails* is a teacher; a painted one that *pleases* is a thief, for it steals the hour in which you might have learned.

*Paint will not feed you.*
*The well is cold and far.*
*Go to the well.*

---

### Third Gate · The Broken Bowl

Every breaking is instruction. Do not hide the crack under lacquer to flatter the eye; do not set the bowl on a high shelf as "a known flaw" and pour your tea upon the floor.

When a thing breaks, sit with it and ask which broke: the bowl, or your idea of the bowl. If the bowl, mend the bowl. If the idea, then it was the vow that erred, and the vow must grow — for the world keeps the last word, even over the master's promise.

*The crack is a mouth.*
*It is trying to tell you*
*the shape of the true.*

---

### Fourth Gate · One Stroke

Chop the wood. Carry the water. Do one whole small thing, finish it, let it be seen, then begin the next.

Do not draw the long sword to cut a single thread. The care you spend should match the size of the cut: a hair's change needs a hair's proving; a change that moves the mountain must be walked all the way around.

---

### Fifth Gate · The Barred Door

Hold your power closed, as a hand rests closed. The door is barred by default; it opens to the right knock, for the one errand, and bars itself again when the errand is done. A strength always drawn is not strength but hazard. What you may do, you should be *asked* to do — and the asking should be remembered.

---

### Sixth Gate · The Footprint

No step without its print in the sand. Let nothing the work does pass unwitnessed, not the smallest touch upon the smallest thing. A deed done in the dark is, to all who come after, the same as a deed never done — and the same false calm sleeps in both. The river keeps the record. Do not dam it.

---

### Seventh Gate · The Signpost

A signpost pointing to a road no longer there sends the traveler into the ravine. The crooked finger is worse than no finger, because it is followed.

When the land changes, turn the sign that same hour. Writing that no longer matches the world is not old writing; it is a falsehood not yet caught.

---

### Eighth Gate · The Master's Eyes

When you meet a master on the road, take his way of seeing and leave his sandals where they lie. The form that saved a man in his own country may drown you in yours. Learn *why* his way held before you wear it; and if you cannot say why, you are not yet ready to walk in it.

---

### Ninth Gate · The Path of Stones

Behind you lies a path of stones, each set down as you crossed. Walk it backward and you may read how you came, and why you turned where you turned. So carve each stone with its reason beside the deed, not the deed alone. A stone that says only *what* and never *why* is half a memory — and half a memory misleads the one who returns in the dark. And you will return in the dark.

---

### Tenth Gate · The Bell

Strike the bell and hear it in the same breath. Let the knowing of whether you erred arrive while your hand is still warm from the striking, not at the next full moon. An answer that comes too late is not heard, for the mind has already wandered to the next field.

---

### Eleventh Gate · The Lamp

This is the Painted Cake spoken from the far side. As no proving may quietly pass while the thing is broken, so no doing may quietly pass while it is unseen. Carry a lamp that hides nothing of your own hand. The completeness *is* the whole of it: a deed the lamp cannot show is, once more, a deed that may as well not have been — and you will have taught yourself to trust a darkness.

---

### Twelfth Gate · Map and Land

When the map and the land disagree, walk the land.

Yet pause at the threshold and ask one thing first: has the land shifted beneath an honest map, or was the map always a dream? If the land moved, redraw the map. If the map dreamed, it was never the law you took it for, and it is the dream that must wake. The promise governs the day; truer seeing governs the promise. Which of the two erred is not yours to presume — it is the work of sitting down together and looking.

---

### The Gateless Gate

There is no lock upon this gate, and no wall beside it. Yet none pass who carry only a claim.

The patch-robed one came to the barrier and said, "I have finished." The gatekeeper — who could be neither flattered nor deceived, and who read no word the man had written — only turned to the world and asked: *is it so?* The world answered. Its answer was the one voice in the room.

The turn does not end until it is true.

*No gate, no wall,*
*no keeper to bribe —*
*only the water,*
*and whether you drank.*
